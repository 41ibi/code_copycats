'Tis a little place con- taining 25 Houfes Houses , without any Walls, Fortifications, or Garriſon, diſtant 80 Leagues from Buenos Ayres Northward, Situate upon the River de la Plata, large Veſſels might come up to it, were it not for a great bank that obstructs the pas- sage a little above Buenos Ayres. Ne- vertheleſs 'tis a very advantageous Poſt, because 'tis the only passage from Peru, Chili and Tucuman to Paraguay, and in a manner the Magazine of the Commodi- ties drawn from thence, particularly that Herb already ſpoken spoken of, which they cannot be without in thoſe Provinces. The Soil is as good and Fertile here as at Buenos Ayres ; and the Town having nothing remarkably different from what has been observ'd observed of Buenos Ayres, I leave it, and proceed upon my Jour- ney. 'Tis counted 140 Leagues from Buenos Ayres to Cordoua, and becauſe because ſome parts of the Road are uninhabited for a long way together; I ſurnished my felf self at my departure with what I was inform'd I fhould should ftand stand in need of; ſo I ſet out having a Savage for my Guide, with Three Hofes Horses and Three Mules, ſome to carry my Baggage, and the reft rest to change upon the way when that I rid upon was tired. From Buenos Ayres, to the River Lu can, and even as far as the River Recife, 30 Leagues, I pass’d by several Habita tions and Farms cultivated by the Spani- ards, but beyond Recife to the River Salladillo, I faw saw none. Let it be obſer- ved by the way, that theſe Rivers as well as all the reſt in the Provinces of Buenos Ayres, Paraguay and Tucuman, that fall into the River de la Plata are fordable on horfe horse -back; but when the Rains or any other accident ſwellsswells them, a Traveller muſt must either ſwim swim over, or elſe else get upon a bundle in the nature of a raft, which a Savage hawls over to the other ſide side. I could not Swim, and ſo so was forc'd to make uſe use of this expedient twice or thrice when I could not find a Ford. The way was this, my kill'd a Wild Bull, flead the Hide off, ſtuffed stuffed it with ſtraw straw, and ty'd it up in a great bundle with thongs of the ſame same Hide, upon which I plac'd my ſelf self with my Baggage ; he ſwamswam over hawling me after him by a Cord ty'd to the bundle, and then he repaſs'd and ſwam swam my Horſes Horses and Mules over to me. All the Country between the River Recife and Salladillo, tho' uninhabited, abounds in Cattle and all ſorts of Fruit trees, except Walnut and Chesnut-trees; there are whole Woods of Nectarine-Trees, Three or Four Leagues in compaſscompass, that bear excellent Fruit, which they not only eat raw, but bake them, or dry them in the Sun, to keep, as we in France do Prunes: They ſeldom uſeseldom use any other than the Wood of this Tree for common firing at Buenos Ayres, and thereabouts.
